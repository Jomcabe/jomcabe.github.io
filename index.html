Understanding the Fourier Slice Theorem

5 March 2026

The Fourier Slice Theorem (also known as the Projection-Slice Theorem) is a fundamental principle underpinning medical imaging modalities like CT scans. In simple terms, it states that the 1D Fourier transform of a parallel projection of a 2D object is exactly equal to a 1D slice of the 2D Fourier transform of that object through its origin.

Mathematically, let's represent an object's density as a 2D function $ f(x, y) $. If we take a projection of this object along the y-axis, we integrate over $ y $ to get a 1D function $ p(x) $:

$$ p(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x, y) \, dy $$

Next, we take the 1D Fourier transform of this projection:

$$ P(u) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} p(x) e^{-i 2\pi u x} \, dx $$

Now, consider the 2D Fourier transform of the original object $ f(x, y) $:

$$ F(u, v) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x, y) e^{-i 2\pi (ux + vy)} \, dx \, dy $$

If we examine the slice of this 2D transform exactly along the x-axis (where $ v = 0 $), the equation simplifies perfectly to match our 1D transform of the projection:

$$ F(u, 0) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \left( \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x, y) \, dy \right) e^{-i 2\pi u x} \, dx = P(u) $$

Because of this relationship, we can take physical projections of an object from multiple angles, compute their 1D Fourier transforms to build out a 2D frequency domain plane, and then apply an inverse 2D Fourier transform to reconstruct the original object's internal structure.

Custom Air Hockey Table

Edgerton Makerspace · MIT

Fig. IV.i — Air hockey table with beam-break scoring, MIT Edgerton Makerspace. Watch on YouTube →

In this project, Diego Salcedo and I were both in the same Edgerton section for Interphase Edge. We decided to build an air hockey table with a unique V shape. After going through many iterations, we found that it would be virtually impossible to create what we wanted within a very short timespan while taking many difficult courses.

Instead, we built our own air hockey table from scratch — Diego working on the machining aspects, myself on the electronics. I configured a 120V AC OEM blower to the table and hooked it up to power and an e-stop to push air through the many holes Diego drilled. I then used a protoboard and infrared sensors to create a beam-break scoring sensor and a scoreboard to track the match.

All in all, this is the most fun I've had building a project.

8.012 Final Project

Classical Mechanics · Ring & Beads Experiment

Fig. IV.ii-a — Physical demonstration of the ring-and-beads experiment.

Fig. IV.ii-b — Simulation with exaggerated parameters to clarify the phenomenon.

Download full report (PDF) →

This was my final project for 8.012, centered on a problem from a problem set that felt deeply counterintuitive even when the math checked out. We built a physical model and coded a simulation to see whether the phenomenon was real and whether it could be explained more intuitively. The problem: two beads on a ring. Theory predicts that if the beads exceed a certain mass, the ring will "jump" at a specific angle. After hours of building models and digging up ancient scales, we confirmed it — and seeing it happen made it feel real.

We submitted the report and were selected to present in front of the entire class. Shoutout to Kaku & Ishan.

UROP: Optimal Orientation for VAM

Volumetric Additive Manufacturing · Light Dose Optimization

Fig. V.iii — Volumetric Additive Manufacturing process, visualized.

This is a current research project on Volumetric Additive Manufacturing (VAM) — a 3D printing technique in which a projector delivers a sequence of 2D images into a rotating vat of photosensitive resin. Each degree of rotation receives a new projection; together they form a 3D object. What I'm investigating is whether the print orientation (φ and θ) meaningfully affects print quality through variations in light dosage. I'll continue updating this section as the work develops.
